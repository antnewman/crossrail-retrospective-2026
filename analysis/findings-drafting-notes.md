## Note 1: Real-terms cost overrun, relocated from the cash headline

**Related drift rows:** assumption 1 (two rows, residual range £2.5-3.7bn at 15-25%).

**Claim.** Cash overrun headlines (the +28% figure reported in PAC-CROSSRAIL-2021) conflate construction-sector inflation with real-terms cost growth. For a programme spanning the high-inflation 2014-2020 period, approximately a quarter of the cash overrun is explained by sector inflation rather than by real-terms cost drift. The real-terms residual of £2.5-3.7bn is still material; the finding is not that the overrun is smaller than reported, but that a meaningful portion of the cash figure is sector inflation rather than programme mismanagement.

**Usage.** Likely opening of the article. The cash-vs-real gap is quantified by the investigation and is the kind of finding that a journalist can carry without having to explain the full methodology.

**Caveats to carry.** The magnitude depends on index choice (ONS-OPI vs BCIS-TPI) and pre-2014 correction; the 15-25% range reflects the second of these but not the first. Under caveat 1, the direction (positive residual) is reported as robust across defensible methodological choices, though whether BCIS would also show a positive residual is not independently verified by this investigation.

## Note 2: Revenue drift decomposes multiplicatively into demand and yield

**Related drift rows:** assumption 3 (single row, drift -37% headline, with -12% demand and -28% yield in notes).

**Claim.** Revenue drift on fare-funded public services is a multiplicative composition of demand drift (journey volumes) and yield drift (average fare per journey). A finding that reports only the aggregate revenue drift is under-specified, because the two components have different causal drivers and different policy implications. For Crossrail in 2024/25 against the 2019 TfL forecast, the -37% revenue drift decomposes such that yield per journey accounts for the larger share of the shortfall.

**Implicit causal direction (NOT to publish as finding without further evidence).** The yield-dominant split points toward fare-policy questions (Mayoral fare freezes, Elizabeth Line fare structure relative to Underground) rather than demand-recovery questions (pandemic impact on journey volumes). This is a hypothesis generated by the decomposition, not a finding the investigation can directly substantiate. It suggests a follow-up question for a future investigation or for Laura's review.

**Usage.** Likely a distinct finding within the article, probably the second most prominent after note 1. Requires framing that distinguishes the observation (multiplicative composition) from the hypothesis (fare policy dominates).

**Caveats to carry.** Definitional match of "passenger income" between the TfL QPR and the 2019 forecast is not yet verified (flagged for Laura). Pre-pandemic baseline means both components reflect pandemic impact.

## Note 3: Near-offsetting effects on the BCR

**Related drift rows:** assumption 4 (three rows, restated BCR 1.88 to 2.06 across scenarios).

**Claim.** The Crossrail restated BCR sits close to the 2011 baseline of 1.97 across the three defensible cost treatments: within a range of approximately 1.88 to 2.06, the baseline is central. The combination of real-terms cost overrun (from assumption 1) and real-terms demand uplift (from assumption 2) produces near-offsetting effects on the BCR. A programme reported as having "overrun by 28%" in cash terms remains, on any defensible real-terms treatment, in broadly the same value-for-money category as originally forecast.

**Usage.** Potentially the headline finding. Combines the cost and demand drifts into a single value-for-money outcome that is counter-intuitive given the cash-overrun framing in the public record. Candidate article conclusion.

**Caveats to carry.** Low confidence. Benefits-linear-in-demand is a simplification. Three co-equal scenarios span a range that straddles the medium/high boundary, meaning the qualitative finding (band change or no band change) depends on methodological choices within the 5-10% pre-2014 correction range. Assumption 2's demand drift is a lower bound because of ramp-up incompleteness and deferred decomposition. A full NPV reconstruction would be needed to defend this claim strongly; the investigation does not provide one.

**Tension with note 1.** Note 1 says there is a real-terms overrun of £2.5-3.7bn. Note 3 says the value-for-money outcome is broadly unchanged. Both are true and the tension is informative: the cost overrun is real, but the demand overrun offsets it in value-for-money terms. The article framing should name this tension explicitly rather than present either finding alone.

## Note 4: Investigation's wider methodological observations

**Carried forward into methodology.md** as generalisable observations (not as Crossrail findings):
- Ramp-up understating (section 3.2): steady-state forecasts compared against non-steady-state outturn systematically understate positive drift.
- Categorical-from-continuous (section 3.5): drift analyses that map continuous metrics through discrete banding frameworks should report continuous drift as the primary finding, not the categorical consequence.
